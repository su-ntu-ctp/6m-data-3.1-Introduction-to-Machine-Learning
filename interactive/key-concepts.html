II.
A definition

ML is "show, don't tell" programming.

Classical programming hands the computer rules and waits for answers. Machine Learning hands the computer answers and asks for the rules. Same problem, opposite direction.

Classical · Rule-based

You write the rules.

def sentiment(text):
    if "love" in text: return "+"
    if "hate" in text: return "-"
    if "never again" in text:
        return "-"
    # ...4,000 more lines...
    return "?"

Works for tidy, deterministic problems. Brittle when language is messy: "yeah right, great service 🙄".

vs.
Machine Learning

The computer learns them.

# 50,000 reviews, already labelled
X, y = load_labelled_reviews()

model = SentimentClassifier()
model.fit(X, y)   # learns the rules

# Predict on Sarah's 10,000 new ones
labels = model.predict(new_reviews)

You don't write the rules — the model induces them from labelled examples. Robust to sarcasm, emoji, spelling mistakes.

IV.
A workflow

The seven steps every real ML project walks through.

Beginners imagine training is the work. In practice, framing, collection, and cleaning swallow most of the calendar. The bar below is to scale — click each step.

Frame · Collect · Clean   ≈ 65% Train · Evaluate   ≈ 18% Deploy · Monitor   ≈ 17%
1

Frame

≈ 25% · highest leverage

Pin down: what's the question, who acts on the answer, what is success, and what's the cost of being wrong each way? A perfect model solving the wrong problem is worth zero.

2

Collect

≈ 18%

Find or build the labelled dataset. For Sarah: 50,000 historic reviews already tagged "+" / "-" sit in the warehouse.

3

Clean

≈ 22%

De-duplicate, fix encodings, handle missing values, strip PII. The least glamorous step, but skipping it poisons everything downstream.

4

Train

≈ 10%

Fit a model on the training split. This is the only step most beginners associate with "doing ML" — yet it's typically a tenth of the calendar.

5

Evaluate

≈ 8% · unseen data only

Score the model on a held-out test set. A 99% training score and a 60% test score means you've memorised — overfitting. Always use unseen data.

6

Deploy

≈ 10%

Package, version, and wire the model into the place it serves predictions — batch script, API, dashboard, or app.

7

Monitor

≈ 7% · forever

Watch for data drift and performance decay. Fraudsters change tactics; customer language shifts. "Train once and forget" does not work — schedule retraining.

The 60-70% rule

If your project plan has training as the biggest box on the Gantt chart, the plan is wrong. Framing, collecting, and cleaning together take the majority of the time on every real ML project.

The holdout commandment

Evaluation must use unseen data. A model that scores 99% on training data routinely scores 60% on data it has never seen — that gap is the only honest number.

V.
A distinction

Training is slow. Inference is fast.

Training is the often-slow, compute-heavy process of learning the rules. Inference is using the trained model to label a new example. Sarah only does inference this week — someone else trained the model.

Training · once, slow, expensive

Learn the rules.

Many labelled examples flow in. The model adjusts its internal weights until predictions match the labels.

VI.
Check yourself

Ten questions from the field notebook.

Try each one mentally first. Click to flip the card and read the sample answer. The numbering matches lesson.md.

▸ Click to reveal Rule-based or ML? Send a coupon to every customer whose birthday is today.
Rule-based. "Is today equal to their birthday?" is a one-line if statement. No data, no uncertainty. An ML model here adds cost and risk for zero benefit.
▸ Click to reveal A bank has millions of labelled fraud transactions. Which "ML is right" condition is trickiest?
They satisfy #1 (patterns too varied to hand-code) and #2 (lots of labels). #3 is the tricky one: false negatives miss fraud (expensive); false positives block real customers (annoying). Choosing the right error trade-off is the design call we revisit in L03.
▸ Click to reveal Training vs inference — what's the difference?
Training learns rules from labelled examples (slow, compute-heavy). Inference uses the trained model on new data — every time it returns a prediction. Sarah only does inference this week; someone else trained the model.
▸ Click to reveal (a) count reviews per month · (b) join reviews with orders · (c) classify a new review. Which module is each?
(a) M1 — summarising the past. (b) M2 — moving / joining data. (c) M3 — judgement on a new thing, no simple rule. Only (c) needs Machine Learning.
▸ Click to reveal Sarah has 50,000 past reviews labelled positive / negative. Which category?
Supervised learning. She has features (the review text) and labels ("+" / "-"). The goal is to predict labels on new reviews.
▸ Click to reveal Newspaper wants to discover topics across 10 years of articles, without naming them. Which category?
Unsupervised. No labels. The goal is to discover structure. We cover this properly in L05.
▸ Click to reveal Could Sarah's task be done unsupervised? What changes?
Yes. She could cluster the 10,000 reviews into, say, 5 groups by similarity. Faster (no labels needed), but less useful — she wouldn't know which cluster is "positive" without inspecting samples.
▸ Click to reveal Priya says "90% accuracy is fine." Is the framing complete?
No. Open questions: accuracy on what — overall or on rare "severe" cases? Who acts on it — does a false positive cost real money? Is topic tagging in scope? Push back is part of the job; reframing is the work.
▸ Click to reveal Training score 98%, test score 70%. Diagnosis?
Classic overfitting — the model memorised the training data instead of learning general patterns. Try a simpler model, more regularisation, more data, or fewer / better features. Covered properly in L03 & L04.
▸ Click to reveal Fraud model deployed; six months later accuracy drops 10%. What happened?
Likely data drift — fraudsters changed tactics, or customer behaviour shifted. Step 7 (monitor) caught the decay; Step 4 (train) needs to run on fresh data. This is why "train once and forget" never works.
